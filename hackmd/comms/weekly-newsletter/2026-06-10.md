## 2) Development Updates (Technical)

### Eliza Cloud infrastructure & deployment hardening
A lot of momentum went into making **Eliza Cloud** more reliable, cheaper to operate, and closer to “turnkey” for production users:

- **Cost optimization:** dedicated tenant-DB nodes were made **optional**, reducing fixed costs for low-density apps.
- **Scaling unblockers:** implemented a **multi-project Hetzner deployment pattern** to bypass server quota limits.
- **Terraform hardening:** workflows were improved with **fail-closed variables** and **state locking**, reducing the chance of partial or unsafe applies.
- **Reliability fixes:** improved infrastructure stability via **GHA control-plane management** and server-type default fixes.

### App deployment & database lifecycle improvements
Several pieces that matter a lot in production—cleanup, teardown, ingress—were pushed forward:

- **Opt-in per-tenant databases** enabled, including **automated teardown and slot release** when an app is deleted.
- **Verified custom domains** are now integrated into the apps ingress stack.
- **Autoscaler efficiency improvements** (including environment-overridable buffer settings and better tier labeling).
- Tooling got love too: the **cloud-sdk route-coverage audit tool** was repaired, and **drift protection** was added for apps data-plane VMs (ignore VM name changes).

### Still in progress / known blockers
A few “last-mile” items are actively being worked:

- Infrastructure fixes around **pgbouncer**, ports, and environment contracts.
- **Steward OAuth PKCE** work across Feed + login surfaces.
- **Billing + ingress** cleanup work, plus tenant DB teardown edge cases.
- A key blocker called out this week: **Google OAuth redirect allowlisting** at the tenant level is still preventing progress (tracked in **issue #8257**).
- Additional launch coordination remains for daemon restarts and scaling transition (tracked in **issue #8321**).

### Proposal: Runtime Open Federation
A new proposal was filed for **Runtime Open Federation**: a permissionless multi-agent network where agents can earn **USDC rewards**. This is early-stage, but it’s an important signal about where “agents as economic actors” could go next.

## 3) Community Spotlight (Discord)

### Third-party registry: smooth submission + clear process
- **naked_monk** submitted **PR #8294** to add `@usenami/plugin-signer`. The submission is a great example of the “do it right” path: correct repo, correct folder, develop branch, CI passing.
- **odilitime** clarified that third-party plugins should be submitted directly to **`elizaOS/eliza`** (not a separate repo), and that **Shaw’s agent** typically reviews/merges registry entries with an ~**48 hour** turnaround.

### Agent builders: companions, voice, and multimodal UX
- **thaoryel** outlined an AI companion stack using **OpenRouter (GLM-5)**, **ElevenLabs** voice, and **Nano Banana 2** image generation, aiming to integrate into a personal Discord server.
- Community confirmed ElizaOS supports Discord, voice (via plugins), and image generation workflows; a “Ruby” agent example in Cozy was referenced as a proof-of-capability for richer media.

### Trading + security: measurement and fraud detection
- **builderradmin** shared a thoughtful benchmarking approach for trading agents using a harness with a single `decide()` function and hidden forward-test periods. One practical takeaway: **simple risk-off strategies** often outperform complex leveraged momentum during real down days.
- **paulf76** introduced **Cabal-Hunter**, an on-chain funding lineage tracer offered as a pay-per-query MCP server (**$0.05 USDC/query**) and integrable with ElizaOS via `@hugen/plugin-x402-solana`. The emphasis on *wallet funding coordination* (not just contract audits) resonated with builders focused on real-world risk.

### Deeper technical attention: Discord voice auditing
- **doniyorv7503** reported auditing `@elizaos/plugin-discord`, focusing on `voice.ts` and real-time voice event + text synchronization behavior under load—exactly the kind of scrutiny that prevents “works in dev, breaks in prod” voice experiences.
